Collection Overview

Sampson County was established in 1784 from Duplin County. Some early court records are missing. Partial causes may have been the actions of Federal sympathizers in 1865 and a clerk's office fire in 1921.

The deed books of Duplin County prior to 1784 are among the records of Sampson County.

Contents of the Collection

1. Bonds, Licenses, Oaths, and Registrations

Scope and Content:

This series may include Apprentice Bonds, Bastardy Bonds, Official Bonds and Records of county officials, Oaths taken by county officials, and other miscellaneous bonds and records pertaining to legal responsibilities of service granted through the courts, as well as Assumed Business Names, Corporations, and Partnerships and Brands or Marks registered in the county and Licenses and Registrations of merchants and professionals kept by the county.

Bastardy Bonds and Records, 1835-1924

Scope and Content:

Includes volumes and boxes of bastardy bonds, arranged chronologically. Other papers may include presentments against and examinations of unwed mothers and mothers-to-be, warrants to bring the putative fathers to court, and receipts for payments made on behalf of bastard children.

Records of the Wardens of the Poor, 1785-1824

Scope and Content:

Records concerning the care of the poor and infirm, a function of the counties before it was assumed by the State Board of Charities and Public Welfare in 1917. In colonial times, care for the poor was an ecclesiastical responsibility; records from this period may be found in vestry minutes. From 1777 to 1868, maintenance of the unfortunate was entrusted to the Wardens of the Poor; after 1868, the Board of County Commissioners assumed the responsibility. Volumes may include vestry minutes, minutes and accounts of the Wardens of the Poor, accounts and registers of the county home, and accounts of outside poor (impoverished persons living at home rather than in the county home).

Other terms for these records: Parish Books or Parish Accounts.

Arrangement:

Chronological

3. Court Records

Scope and Content:

This series includes records pertaining to the administration and actions of the county court system, particularly Civil and Criminal Actions before a jury and other actions before the court or the clerk, such as Special Proceedings. The dominant court prior to 1868 was known as the Court of Pleas and Quarter Sessions; after court reform circa 1868, it was known as the Superior Court.

For court records concerning land and property matters, see also series 4, Land and Property Records. For records pertaining to Wills and Estate matters, see also series 5, Estates Records and Wills. For Adoptions, see also series 6, Marriage, Divorce and Vital Statistics. Series 10, subseries 928, may also contain loose court records.

Minute Dockets, Court of Pleas and Quarter Sessions and Superior Court, 1794-1968

Scope and Content:

The minute dockets record the daily transactions of the court, generally recording judgments in civil and criminal actions or documenting non-juried matters. They also reflect the internal workings of the court, recording the appointment and qualification of clerks, the summoning and impaneling of juries, and the hearing of grand jury reports.

Appearance and Reference Dockets, Court of Pleas and Quarter Sessions and Superior Court, 1821-1868

Scope and Content:

Records include Appearance Dockets for the Court of Pleas and Quarter Sessions and Superior Court, which list civil cases to appear for any other purpose than jury trial, including preliminary proceedings, and document issuance of writs, summonses, and subpoenas as well as appeals from lower courts and proceedings by petition. Dockets show names of parties, nature of action, and summaries of pleadings or proceedings. Later Appearance Dockets usually were merely lists of persons bonded or otherwise held to appear before court in criminal matters, including persons on probation and material witnesses.

Prior to 1820, both Appearance Dockets and Reference Dockets were often kept in same volume with Trial Dockets, which are listed in subseries CV, Civil Dockets, if available.

Appearance Docket, Court of Pleas and Quarter Sessions, 1821-1868

Scope and Content:

Record of issuance of writs, summonses and subpoenas to bring defendants and witnesses to following term of court. Also records appearances of appeals from magistrates' courts and of proceedings by petition (sometimes recorded in separate Petitions Docket). Columnar arrangement shows number of case and name or initials of attorneys; names of parties; form of action; disposition of writ; and any rule or action taken by court at term, including confessed judgments. Prior to 1820, appearance dockets were often kept in same volume with trial dockets.

Volumes span the following dates: 1821-1833 1833-1842 1853-1861 1861-1868

Judgment/Execution Dockets, Court of Pleas and Quarter Sessions and Superior Court, 1800-1946

Scope and Content:

Equity Execution Dockets and Execution Dockets record the names of all parties involved in compensatory judgments awarded by the Court of Equity or Superior Court. After 1868, in most counties this information was recorded in Judgment Dockets, although some continued to use separate Execution Dockets.

Civil Dockets, Court of Pleas and Quarter Sessions and Superior Court, 1784-1927

Scope and Content:

Record of civil causes expected to come to trial during term. Brings forward information from Appearance Docket and also shows pleadings of defendant, prior to ruling by court, verdict, judgment, and final orders. In most counties prior to 1820, Appearance, Trial, and Reference Dockets were maintained in one civil docket at the convenience of the clerk. With the proliferation of cases after 1820, most clerks began keeping the various civil dockets in separate volumes. After 1868, this information was recorded in Civil Issues Dockets, including names of parties, history of cases carried forward from Summons Docket, and note of proceedings at trial. See also series AR, Appearance and Reference Docket.

Inebriates, Lunacy, and Judicial Hospitalizations Records, 1900-1968

Scope and Content:

Records include documentation of hearings for commitment of insane persons and inebriates, listing the name of the alleged lunatic, oaths of parties testifying to his/her lunacy, and copies of the clerk's order of commitment and the superintendent of the state hospital's order of release. Papers may include appointment of guardians to oversee the property of the unfit. After 1899, the clerk was required to keep lunacy matters in a distinct volume, entitled Record of Inquisition of Lunacy or simply Lunacy Docket. From 1868 to 1899, lunacy proceedings were recorded in the Orders and Decrees (922).
See also Court Records subseries 922, Orders and Decrees and Special Proceedings.

4. Land and Property Records

Scope and Content:

This series contains records related to ownership, transfer, and taxing of land and property.

Deeds, 1926-1956

Scope and Content:

Records are unprocessed. Records include original deeds left in the courthouse after being deposited with the clerk for probate and recording. These loose deeds can include various types such as deeds of sale, deeds of trust, mortgage deeds, and quitclaim (or release) deeds. Within these records, the seller is commonly referred to as the grantor, the buyer as grantee.

Access Restriction:

Records stored in the State Records Center require additional time to pull. Access to these records on Saturdays requires prior arrangements. Please consult the reference staff for more information.

Estates/Accounts, Dockets, 1830-1969

Scope and Content:

Contains bound volumes of account, inventory, and/or settlement records related to the administration and settling of deceased persons' property. These include Record of Estates (pre-1868), Record of Accounts (post-1868), Accounts of Sales and Resales of Estates, Record of Inheritance Tax, Inventories of Estates, Amounts Paid Indigent Children, and Record of Settlements. Prior to 1868, volumes entitled record of estates may include inventories, accounts and final settlements of estates, and guardians' accounts. After 1868, these volumes in most counties contain only inventories, accounts of sales and annual accounts of administrators and executors.

8. Wills

Scope and Content:

Adams-Ziegler. Includes record of wills and original wills arranged alphabetically by decedent. Many of the older record of wills may also record estates matters (inventories, accounts of sale, and final settlements) and deeds. Recording of wills on a regular basis began about 1820. At a later date, wills that had been proved prior to 1820 as well as wills that had not be probated were entered into a record book identified as Will Book A. Excerpt from North Carolina Wills: A Testator Index, 1665-1900, by Thornton W. Mitchell (Raleigh: 1987).

Scope and Content:

This series may include Armed Forces Discharges; Cemetery Records; Coroner's Inquests and Reports; Military Records; Pension Records; Personal Accounts; Road Records and Reports; School Records; and truly miscellaneous records collected from various county agencies and offices. Miscellaneous Records boxes may also include records from all other county series that were of insufficient quantity to make a full box.

Road and Railroad Records, 1829-1921

Scope and Content:

Road Records may include volumes or boxes of records concerning appointment of road overseers, reports on condition of roads, and petitions for new roads. Boxes may also include criminal actions concerning negligence of overseers and claims against the county for services on the roads. Records concerning bridges and railroads are also included in this series, usually as separate subseries. Volumes may also include minutes of the county highway or good roads commission.
